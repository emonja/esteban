YO SOY

Discussion & Philosophical Notes — v01

How to Use These Notes

These notes are not meant to be read aloud or delivered verbatim. They exist as anchors for discussion, reflection, and future writing.

The experience always comes first. Language comes after.

Instructors are encouraged to select fragments, questions, or ideas and let discussion emerge organically.

1. Presence Without Introduction

YO SOY replaces verbal self-introduction with sonic presence. Participants are not asked to describe themselves. They are asked to exist.

Discussion Prompts

  • What does it mean to be present without speaking?
  • Did you feel more or less exposed than in a verbal introduction?
  • Was the sound “you”, or something external?

Language often performs identity rather than revealing it. Sound here does not represent the self — it occupies space.

2. Claiming Space vs Asking Permission

Participants are instructed to use device speakers and raise volume. This instruction is symbolic, not technical.

Discussion Prompts

  • How did it feel to be audible to everyone?
  • Did you hesitate before raising the volume?
  • Did you feel you were bothering others?

Personal devices usually privatize experience. Headphones create isolation bubbles.

YO SOY temporarily reverses this logic: sound becomes public again, and space must be negotiated.

3. Involuntary Contribution and Ethical Friction

No one “plays” the instrument. Everyone contributes by being present.

Discussion Prompts

  • Did you feel in control of your contribution?
  • Did you want to stop at any point?
  • Did you feel responsible for the collective sound?

Many real-world systems work this way: participation is not optional, only influence varies.

YO SOY makes this structure audible.

4. Individuality Inside a Collective Field

Each participant produces a different sound, but no sound is privileged.

Discussion Prompts

  • Could you clearly distinguish your own sound?
  • Did individuality matter once the room filled?
  • Did you listen to others, or just perceive noise?

Individuality often assumes isolation to be legible. In a shared field, difference exists but salience dissolves.

5. Agency vs Control

Participants answer questions, but the system does not fully obey.

Discussion Prompts

  • Did the instrument feel responsive to your choices?
  • Did you trust the system to reflect you accurately?
  • How did you react when it didn’t?

This exposes a key distinction: agency is the feeling of influence, control is actual power over outcomes.

6. The Glitch as Meaning

Certain system failures are documented rather than fixed.

Discussion Prompts

  • Did you notice inconsistencies?
  • Did you assume they were intentional?
  • Did they change your trust in the system?

Glitches reveal hidden assumptions and system boundaries. They are epistemological events, not just technical errors.

7. Silence Is Not Neutral

Once the system starts, silence is no longer available.

Discussion Prompts

  • Was silence possible?
  • Did silence feel like absence or refusal?
  • Who benefits from silence in shared systems?

Silence is often framed as neutral, but it can function as privilege, withdrawal, or protection.

8. No Failure, No Performance

There is no correct output and no evaluation.

Discussion Prompts

  • Did the lack of success criteria feel freeing or unsettling?
  • Were you waiting for feedback?
  • Did you feel exposed anyway?

Removing performance often reveals how deeply performance logic is internalized.

9. Data, Memory, and Recognition

The system remembers participants, but recognition is subtle.

Discussion Prompts

  • Does being stored mean being recognized?
  • Is memory the same as acknowledgment?
  • Who decides when memory matters?

Digital systems excel at storage, not meaning. A system can remember you perfectly and still ignore you.

10. Toward Stage 2

Stage 1 centers individuals. Future stages will remove that center.

Speculative Prompts

  • What happens when control is taken away?
  • Should individuals consent to losing agency?
  • Can collectives act ethically?

The discomfort of Stage 1 is preparation, not accident.

Instructor Note

Do not rush interpretation. Let discomfort sit before analysis. Avoid explaining intent too early.

Your role is not to resolve meaning, but to host reflection.
